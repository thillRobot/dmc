\section{\sectiontitleII}

\frame{
\frametitle{\sectiontitleII}


\small

A mass moves in the x direction with only the force of gravity acting on it. Netwon's Second Law combined with the definition of differential work done  by a force through a distance gives a relation between kinetic energy and work done by the external force.

\begin{multicols}{2}

\includegraphics[scale=.4]{conservation_energy_fig1.png}

\scalebox{\eqscl}{${\bf f}=m{\bf a}=m\frac{d {\bf v}}{dt}$} \vspace{1mm}\\
\scalebox{\eqscl}{$dW={\bf f}d{\bf x}$}\vspace{1mm}\\
\scalebox{\eqscl}{$dW={\bf f}d{\bf x}=m{\bf a}d{\bf x}=md{\bf v}\frac{d{\bf x}}{dt}=md{\bf v}{\bf v}$}\vspace{1mm}\\
\scalebox{\eqscl}{$\rightarrow dW=mv_xdv_x$}\vspace{1mm}\\
\scalebox{\eqscl}{$W_{12}=\int_{x_1}^{x_2} mv_xdv_x=\frac{1}{2}mv_x^2|_{x_1}^{x_2}$}\vspace{3mm}\\
\scalebox{\eqscl}{$W_{12}=\frac{1}{2}mv_{x_2}^2-\frac{1}{2}mv_{x_1}^2=KE_1-KE_2\hspace{5mm}{\B (1)}$}

\end{multicols}

\vspace{1mm}
{\tiny Images: T.Hill}

}

\frame{
\frametitle{\sectiontitleII}

Now, subsitute the distance traveled into the differential work relation and you are left with the familar relation between work done by gravity and the potential energy in the system. 

\begin{multicols}{2}

\includegraphics[scale=.35]{conservation_energy_fig1.png}

\scalebox{\eqscl}{$dW={\bf f}d{\bf x}$}\vspace{1mm}\\
\scalebox{\eqscl}{$W_{12}=\int_{x_1}^{x_2}{\bf f}d{\bf x}=(-mg)|_{x_1}^{x_2}$}\vspace{1mm}\\
\scalebox{\eqscl}{$W_{12}=-mg(x_2-x_1)\hspace{20mm}{\B (2)}$}\vspc

Combine ${\B (1)}$ and ${\B (2)}$. \vspace{1mm}\\

\scalebox{\eqscl}{$W_{12}=-mg(x_2-x_1)=\frac{1}{2}mv_{x_2}^2-\frac{1}{2}mv_{x_1}^2$}\vspc

\end{multicols}

\vspace{1mm}
{\tiny Images: T.Hill}

}

\frame{
\frametitle{\sectiontitleII}

The resulting equation clearly shows the conservation of energy for a 1DOF particle moving in the direction of gravity. This derivation is often shown in more genralized form but this version is easy to understand and will work for our purposes. \vspc
\begin{framed}
\scalebox{1.0}{$ \Delta KE +\Delta PE = 0 $} \hspcc or \hspcc \scalebox{1.0}{$ KE + PE = Constant $} \vspc
with \hspc \scalebox{1.0}{$KE=\frac{1}{2}mv^2$} \hspc and \hspc \scalebox{1.0}{$PE=mgh$} 
\end{framed}
	
While deriving dynamics (EOMs) for many systems the alternate form shown the right will be used.

}
